Threat Intelligence

Threat Intelligence is so vast and conceptual that it really is hard to grasp what exactly it is. Some say, it’s reading and writing reports; others might say, actively looking at the cyberlandscape and determine trends; and some might also not know what exactly what they do.

Here’s my perspective, a good Cyber Threat Intelligence Team (CTI) provides an organization confident research of the landscape to determine better business decisions. Do they research and write reports? Yes. Are they actively looking at cybersecurity trend? Absolutely. But what makes a good CTI Team is the ability to convey those APT and CVE findings into readable reports to executive management. A good CTI Team assess cyber trends and forsees any possibility of those trends affecting the organization.

Generating a high level CTI team from a business perspective takes time, funding, and clear communication between board room and management.

How does one get into CTI? I’m not one to say, because I myself am working toward that goal, but what I have observed in an organizatiion is that CTI teams consist of high-level thinking and writing for starters. Being able to find data, find threats, and to be able to connect the dots is an essential skill. Researching the MITRE ATT&CK framework, knowing different APT TTPs and incorporating them into how it can affect the business is an essential skill.
